UX & Design implications, 2026

Travel at the speed of conversation

AI changes what the interface is for. Instead of optimizing long booking flows, we can design review surfaces, conversational inputs, calm status layers, and clear override patterns. The goal is not abstract automation. The goal is faster understanding, better decisions, and more confident action.

Core design shifts
01
From search to proposal

Users stop initiating. The UI becomes a review surface for AI-generated proposals. Design must communicate confidence, reasoning, and alternatives. Not just options.

02
From flow to conversation

Linear booking funnels give way to dialogue, typed or spoken. Voice becomes a first-class modality: in the car, at the gate, hands-free in a hotel room.

03
Voice as primary surface

Voice UX isn't a feature. It's a distinct modality that needs its own interaction design, error handling, and trust signals alongside the visual interface.

04
From gating to guardrailing

Policy stops being enforced at booking time through hard blocks. It becomes a background constraint the AI reasons within. Exceptions surface as conversations, not errors.

The interfaces that remain after automation are the ones that matter most: communicating AI reasoning, building trust, and handling edge cases gracefully.

Opportunity 07: Voice UX

Voice and visual, working together

Voice doesn't replace the screen. It adds a layer on top of it. A traveler might speak a request hands-free and then see the options rendered visually before confirming. The design challenge is knowing which modality carries which part of the interaction: voice for input and quick confirmations, screen for complex comparisons and high-stakes decisions. The visual UI and the voice UI have to feel like one coherent system.

The core tension

Autonomy vs. agency

The central design question is not how to automate more. It is when to act, when to ask, and how to explain the tradeoff. Good design gives users adjustable autonomy, visible reasoning, and a safe path to intervene without starting over.

Configurable autonomyTrust as a design materialContext-sensitive defaultsReversibility at every step
Design Principles

How we design at Spotnana

These principles guide every interface decision we make — from the smallest interaction to full product strategy. They are how we hold ourselves accountable to the people who use what we build.

01

Clarity over cleverness

Travel is stressful. Our interfaces should reduce cognitive load, not add to it. Every element must earn its place. If a user has to think about the UI, we've failed.

Plain languageVisible labelsPredictable patterns
02

AI assists, humans decide

Intelligence should surface the right option at the right time — but never take away the user's agency. AI recommendations are suggestions, not mandates.

Show reasoningEasy overrideNever auto-book
03

Design for the 80%, accommodate the 20%

Most travelers need a fast, simple path. Power users and admins need depth. Layer complexity progressively — the default experience should be effortless, with advanced controls always accessible.

Smart defaultsProgressive disclosure
04

One experience, many contexts

A traveler, an agent, a TMC admin, and a company policy manager all use Spotnana — often for the same trip. Our design must feel consistent across every persona while adapting to each one's needs.

Shared componentsPersona-specific density
05

Speed is a feature

Every second matters when someone is rebooking a cancelled flight at the airport. Our interfaces must be fast to load, fast to understand, and fast to act on.

Time-to-actionPrefetch next stepsMinimize clicks
06

Trust through transparency

Corporate travel involves money, policy, and compliance. Users must understand why something is allowed, restricted, or recommended. Never leave them guessing.

Inline policy statusFare breakdownsEarly approval signals
07

Meet people where they are

Not every interaction requires opening the app. The best travel experience might be a Slack message, an email confirmation, or a voice command. Design for the medium, not just the screen.

Multi-channelAdapt to surface
08

Ship, learn, refine

Good design is iterative. We prototype rapidly, test with real users, and improve based on evidence — not assumptions. Shipping fast is not the enemy of shipping well.

Prototype earlyReal dataMeasure outcomes
Traveler perspective

The AI-native business trip

A design vision for the traveler surface: fewer search steps, better defaults, and clearer moments of confirmation. The desired outcome is not “magic.” It is a trip flow that feels legible, low-effort, and easy to correct.

Phase 01: Intent detected

The AI knows before you ask

A meeting invite lands for a client in Chicago next Thursday. The AI reads your calendar, cross-references your company travel policy, checks your loyalty status, scans weather and disruption patterns on ORD routes, and surfaces a proposed itinerary before you've thought to open a booking tool.

Agent message

"You have an 8am client meeting Thursday at Willis Tower. I've drafted an itinerary: UA 492 departing 6:05am (aisle, economy plus, within policy), Hyatt Regency on the Riverwalk (preferred vendor, $189, $11 under cap). Confirm to book both."

Smart tradeoffs surfaced

Agent perspective

Designing for agent judgment

This section should be read as an interface problem, not a workforce prediction. If routine tasks are automated, the agent surface should optimize for triage, context handoff, and high-confidence intervention.

What the work looks like
Move agents out of booking forms

The most useful near-term design outcome is simple: reduce repetitive form work and concentrate attention on decisions that need judgment. That means queues, summaries, recommended actions, and fast intervention patterns.

Before AI

High-volume, low-judgment work

Responding to booking requests, searching inventory, applying policy, sending confirmation emails, chasing receipts. Repetitive and not why agents got into the business.

Search & bookPolicy lookupConfirmation emails
After AI

Exception handling and escalation

The AI handles the routine. Agents receive a curated queue of situations that genuinely need human judgment: a stranded traveler, a VIP reroute, a policy exception that needs a conversation.

Exception queueVIP managementReroute orchestration
Design opportunity

Recommendation and intervention tools

We can design agent tools that surface patterns, draft responses, and package context for better human decisions. The desired outcome is faster resolution with less hunting, not a vague promise of “advisory.”

Decision supportPattern detection
UX implications
Designing the agent console

The agent's primary interface is no longer a booking tool. It's a command center for monitoring AI activity, triaging exceptions, and acting on situations that need a human.

Interface shift 01

The exception queue

Agents start their day with an AI-prioritized exception queue. Each item shows the situation, what the AI already tried, why it escalated, and the recommended human action. Good UX means agents can assess and act in seconds, not minutes.

Exception summary

"Traveler J. Park, BOS to LHR, tomorrow 7am. Original UA 56 cancelled. No compliant alternatives within policy found. Client has board meeting 9am London Wednesday. Awaiting agent guidance on policy exception or alternative routing."

Priority triageAI context handoffPolicy exception flow
Interface shift 02

Live traveler monitoring

Agents see a live view of every traveler currently in transit, flagged by risk level: a delayed connection, a traveler in a disrupted city, a missed hotel check-in. The map is ambient; intervention is one click.

Real-time trackingRisk scoringDuty of care
Interface shift 03

Natural language override

When an agent needs to act, they don't navigate booking forms. They instruct the AI in plain language: "Rebook on BA, business class, policy exception approved, notify traveler and update the hotel." The AI executes, the agent confirms.

NL commandsOne-click confirmAudit trail
Desired outcome

Judgment in context

The agent UI should help a person understand the situation quickly, trust the system's prior work, and act without reassembling the case from scratch. That is something we can design today with better summaries, action scaffolds, and audit visibility.

Human judgment at edge casesContext packagingVisible system historyFast intervention

Admin & EA perspective

Policy that enforces itself

This is really a design problem about policy, preference, and oversight. The opportunity is to reduce manual coordination and replace it with interfaces that make rules, exceptions, and traveler wellbeing easier to see and manage.

The EA role reimagined
From manual coordination to preference systems

The design opportunity is not to glorify strategy work. It is to capture preferences, approvals, and edge cases in ways that make future trips easier to review, adjust, and trust.

Work that disappears

Booking and logistics

Searching flights, comparing hotel options, managing approval chains, processing expense reports, sending itineraries. This is the work that consumes most of an EA's travel-related time today. The AI does all of it.

Search & bookApprovalsExpense processing
Work that remains

Judgment and preferences

Knowing the executive prefers an aisle seat on the left side of a 737. Knowing they never take the 6am flight before a board meeting. This institutional knowledge feeds the AI, but the EA is the one who holds and curates it.

Preference curationContext managementAI supervision
Work that grows

Complex coordination

Multi-leg international trips with visas. Team offsites requiring 30 travelers in sync. A last-minute reschedule cascading across 3 time zones and 5 stakeholders. Complex situations that require judgment, persuasion, and accountability.

Complex tripsGroup coordinationCrisis management
The travel manager role
From approval bottlenecks to policy tooling

A strong outcome for travel managers is fewer one-off approvals and better interfaces for shaping policy behavior. The more clearly the system explains exceptions and outcomes, the less manual review it should require.

Interface shift 01

The policy console

Travel policy moves from a static document to a living ruleset. The travel manager's primary interface is a policy configuration tool that lets them define rules in plain language, simulate the spend and compliance impact of changes before publishing, and see in real time where exceptions are occurring and why.

Policy insight

"Your $189 NYC hotel cap generated 47 policy exceptions last month, 31 of which were approved. Average exception spend was $218. Raising the cap to $215 would bring 28 of those exceptions into compliance and save 3.2 hours of approval processing time."

Policy simulationException analyticsApproval automation
Interface shift 02

Spend forecasting

AI can predict travel spend 90 days out based on calendar density, booked trips, and historical patterns. Travel managers can manage budget proactively rather than reporting on actuals after it's spent.

Predictive spendBudget management
Interface shift 03

Traveler wellbeing signals

How many trips per month is the sales team averaging? Who's been on back-to-back weeks of travel for two months straight? Data that was always available but never surfaced, now presented as an actionable wellbeing dashboard.

Travel intensityBurnout signals
Desired outcome

Replace approval chains with better policy UX

Approval chains are often a workaround for weak policy interfaces. A stronger future state is explicit rules, visible reasoning, targeted escalation, and auditability built into the product. Much of that can be designed today. The harder invention work is making those controls feel understandable instead of bureaucratic.
